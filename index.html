Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF)

"The most common cyanotic congenital heart defect – 'blue baby syndrome'." – Pillitteri

TOF Explained Like You Are 5 Years Old

🧸 Imagine the heart is a house with 4 problems:

🔴 Problem #1: A hole between the two big bottom rooms (VSD).
🔵 Problem #2: The door to the lungs is too narrow (Pulmonary stenosis).
🟡 Problem #3: The muscle walls of the bottom right room are extra thick.
🟢 Problem #4: The big door to the body sits right over the hole (Overriding aorta).

Because the door to the lungs is small, some blue (oxygen‑poor) blood goes through the hole into the red side and out to the body – that makes babies look blue (cyanotic).

✅ Doctors can fix these problems with an operation so the child can run, play, and grow healthy!

The 4 defects of Tetralogy of Fallot

1. VSD (hole)
2. Pulmonary stenosis (narrow)
3. RV hypertrophy (thick muscle)
4. Overriding aorta

Definition & Epidemiology

Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF) – a congenital heart defect consisting of four anatomical abnormalities: ventricular septal defect (VSD), pulmonary stenosis, right ventricular hypertrophy, and overriding aorta. It is the most common cyanotic congenital heart defect.

📊 Incidence
Occurs in 3‑6 per 10,000 live births. Accounts for 5‑7% of all congenital heart defects.
🇵🇭 Philippines
TOF is the most common cyanotic CHD seen in Philippine hospitals. Approximately 300‑500 new cases diagnosed annually.
⚠️ Key point: TOF is associated with 22q11.2 deletion (DiGeorge syndrome) in ~15% of cases.

The 4 Defects & Pathophysiology

🔹 Four anomalies:
  1. Ventricular septal defect (VSD)
  2. Pulmonary stenosis (infundibular or valvular)
  3. Right ventricular hypertrophy (RVH)
  4. Overriding aorta (aorta straddles the VSD)
🩸 Hemodynamics
Degree of pulmonary stenosis determines shunt direction. Mild PS → left‑to‑right (acyanotic). Severe PS → right‑to‑left shunt (cyanotic). Deoxygenated blood enters systemic circulation → cyanosis.
💡 Mnemonic for the 4 defects: "PROVe" – Pulmonary stenosis, Right ventricular hypertrophy, Overriding aorta, VSD.

Clinical Features & Tet Spells

🔵 Classic signs
• Cyanosis (bluish skin) – may be present from birth or develop with crying/feeding
• Clubbing of fingers and toes after 6 months
• Systolic ejection murmur at left sternal border (from PS)
• Single S2 heart sound
⚠️ Hypercyanotic spells ("Tet spells")
Sudden episodes of deep cyanosis, tachypnea, irritability, and syncope. Often triggered by crying, feeding, or defecation. The infant assumes the "squatting position" (older child) to increase systemic vascular resistance.
🚨 Emergency management for Tet spell: Knee‑chest position, calm the infant, oxygen, morphine, IV fluids, propranolol, or phenylephrine to increase afterload.

Diagnosis & Tests

  • Echocardiography (Doppler): Gold standard – visualizes all four defects and severity of PS.
  • Chest X‑ray: "Boot‑shaped" heart (coeur en sabot) due to RVH and upturned apex.
  • ECG: Right ventricular hypertrophy, right axis deviation.
  • Pulse oximetry: Decreased oxygen saturation (often 60‑80% in cyanotic episodes).
  • Cardiac catheterization: Pre‑surgical assessment of coronary anatomy and pulmonary arteries.
🔍 X‑ray sign: "Boot‑shaped heart" – classic finding in TOF.

Treatment & Nursing Care

💊 Medical management
• Propranolol (to prevent and treat Tet spells)
• Oxygen during spells
• IV fluids and morphine for prolonged spells
• Iron supplementation (anemia worsens cyanosis)
⚕️ Surgical repair
Complete repair at 4‑6 months of age: patch closure of VSD, resection of pulmonary stenosis (outflow tract patch). Staged approach: palliative shunting (Blalock‑Taussig shunt) in severe cases, followed by complete repair later.
👩‍⚕️ Nursing priorities: Monitor oxygen saturation, prevent dehydration, position for tet spells (knee‑chest), teach parents signs of spell, prepare for surgery, provide emotional support.

Complications & Prognosis

  • Untreated: Progressive cyanosis, polycythemia (increased RBCs), hyperviscosity, brain abscess, stroke, growth retardation, early death (usually by age 20 without surgery).
  • Post‑surgical: Residual pulmonary stenosis, pulmonary regurgitation, arrhythmias (ventricular tachycardia), right heart failure, sudden death (rare).
  • Prognosis: Excellent with complete repair: 95% survival to adulthood. Lifelong cardiology follow‑up required.
📈 Long‑term: Patients with repaired TOF need regular echocardiograms, exercise limitations, and endocarditis prophylaxis for the first 6 months after repair or if residual defects.

Prevention & Family Education

  • Prenatal: Folic acid (400‑800 mcg) preconception; avoid teratogens; control maternal diabetes; screen for 22q11.2 deletion in affected family.
  • Parent teaching: Recognize Tet spells (knee‑chest position, calm infant), administer propranolol as prescribed, monitor feeding and weight, keep well‑hydrated, prevent iron‑deficiency anemia.
  • Endocarditis prophylaxis: Not routinely indicated after complete repair without residual defect, but required for 6 months post‑surgery or if residual shunt.
📞 Parent teaching mnemonic “S.P.E.L.L.” – Squat/knee‑chest, Propranolol, Emergency care, Learn triggers, Look for cyanosis.
